What is the Torsion of a Tangle Toy?

Tangle toys are a popular fidget toy. The standard model is made from 22 quarter circles that clip together at joints that rotate 360 degrees (but do not `bend'). They are quite satisfying to twist and turn and wrap around your fingers, especially if you're a tactile sort of autistic person. But they also represent a reasonably interesting subspace of embedded curves in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \).

This subspace of embedded curves is 22-dimensional - one degree of freedom per joint - assuming that we don't disconnect/reconnect any pieces. That is enough to allow you to make quite a variety of curves, and all the curves are smooth-looking (unlike a polyline) because the limit of the tangents agree on either side of all the joints.

But exactly how smooth are these curves? What is the torsion at each point? What does the TNB frame look like?

The tangent is easy. You just fin the tangent of the current circle.

The normal is also quite easy - we know that the normal to a circular curve embedded in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \) is perpendicular to the plane of the circle, and we find its orientation using the right-hand rule. But then, we quickly see that the normal is not continuous! Because the plane of the circle can change abruptly when we cross a joint.

The same goes for the binormal - which is perpendicular to both the tangent and the normal.

The osculating circles are just the extensions of the quarter-circles. Since all the quarter-circles have the same radius, then the curvature is constant.

Therefore, the `tangle-toy' curves have quite a special property: the curvature is constant but the torsion is not even well-defined.
